Last lesson, we introduced the stages of spoken language development, from birth to five years old and we noted that these stages are ‘universal’, ‘innate’ and part of being human.

This lesson, we will acknowledge that the way that we 'learn' to read and write follows the same stages of development as speech development.

The aim of this lesson is to use a language levels approach to learning to read and write, as detailed in the A.Q.A Scheme of work. Language consists of levels:

    • --Object of study-------------Name of field-------------Size of unit
    • --Language use---------------Pragmatics-----------------Largest
    • --Meaning---------------------Semantics----------------------------
    • --Sentences, clauses---------Syntax---------------------------------
    • --Words, forms---------------Morphology--------------------------
    • --Classified sounds----------Phonology----------------------------
    • --All human sounds---------Phonetics-----------------Smallest ↑

This 'bottom-up' approach shows how the foundations of language are built upon. 
As babies we traversed these stages to acquire our spoken language, we first began 
to verbalise the simple vowel sounds: 
     /a, e, o/,then simple consonants /p, b, m/, (phonetics)
     then consonants and vowels together:
     /pa, ma/, (phonology)
     moved on to words: 
     /mama/, (morphology)
     then sentences: 
     /ta mama/, (syntax)
     to a growing vocabulary and discourse knowledge:
     (semantics)
     and practical elements of speech, such as turn-taking, audience:
     (pragmatics)
This is also the structure we will follow when we learn to read, indeed if we have 
not progressed with our speech levels we will have a hard time progressing thorough 
the levels of learning to read and write and may require intervention, such as, 
speech and language therapy.

When we learn to read and write we begin to associate the sounds we produce with 
their graphic representation /a/ = A. We begin to learn the alphabet and the complex 
way 26 symbols stand for the 44 sounds of the English language, 20 of those 
being vowel sounds here. 

Representation of these sounds is achieved through complex spelling structures or 
‘orthography’. The use of double vowels, for example, ‘oo’, ‘ee’, in spelling,
accounts for extra sounds. 


The different meanings of 'might - mite', ‘sight – site’, show the ‘morphemic’
character of English; that it uses spelling to represent elements of meaning.
We learn rules, such as, adding the past tense morpheme –ed to a word 
to make it known that it occurred in the past.


Once children have learned the sound – letter correspondence, they have a system
for spelling. We learn to put the sounds together to make words, ‘c-a-t’.
Then put the words together to make sentences. Simple sentences become more complex
with growing knowledge of syntactics, grammar and pragmatics.
Lewis Carroll wrote a nonsensical rhyme that appears to make sense because of 
the structures of the English Language.
